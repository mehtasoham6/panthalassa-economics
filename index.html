Model Assumptions

Workload Scope

This model assumes node-independent workloads — inference, fine-tuning, and small-batch training jobs that run entirely within a single node's compute capacity.

  • Panthalassa nodes connect via satellite uplink, not fiber. Inter-node bandwidth is orders of magnitude lower than terrestrial data center interconnects. Tightly-coupled distributed training across multiple nodes — the kind required to train frontier models — is not feasible with this architecture. This constraint also eliminates one of terrestrial data centers' largest capital costs: the GPU interconnect fabric (InfiniBand switches, transceivers, structured cabling), which runs $1.75–4.42/W in a conventional facility. The workload limitation and the cost advantage are two sides of the same coin.
  • Large data transfers (model weights, training datasets) may require physical transport of storage media by vessel, introducing multi-day latency for bulk data movement. The satellite backhaul cost line covers real-time egress only, not bulk ingest.

Both Columns

  • Target capacity is nameplate electrical output at the stated capacity factor.
  • Analysis period is an accounting window, not a service lifetime; no residual value modeled.

Ocean (Panthalassa)

  • GPUs not included in either column — this model covers everything upstream of compute hardware. GPU cost is a pure add-on equal in both scenarios and therefore cancels in comparisons.
  • Sea-state availability is a flat fraction — no Weibull distribution, no storm derate, no seasonal variation.
  • Biofouling/corrosion modeled as a single compounding derate on hull/structural cost; real-world is non-linear and coating-dependent.
  • Self-propulsion and station-keeping treated as zero opportunity cost.
  • No decommissioning, retrieval, or disposal costs.
  • Satellite backhaul priced at flat $/Gbps-yr; no congestion, latency, or coverage-gap modeling.
  • No regulatory, permitting, or insurance cost included.
  • Catastrophic loss rate covers storms, sinking, and unrecoverable electrical failures. Per-node replacement cost = deployment cost + node HW cost. Applied as a compounding annual probability over the analysis period.
  • Spare fleet inflates total nodes procured and deployed beyond the active count needed to meet capacity. Spare nodes contribute to all cost lines (fleet, delivery, ops, catastrophic base) but not to energy output.
  • Supply chain multiplier is a step function applied to Node Fleet and Deployment costs based on total fleet size: baseline (≤200 nodes), 0.85× volume discount (201–500), 0.80× deeper discount (501–2000), 0.90× partial bottleneck erosion (>2000). At very large fleet sizes, the model assumes Panthalassa becomes a price-moving buyer of steel plate and turbine components, partially eroding discounts. The crossover thresholds are illustrative — real dynamics depend on global steel and turbine market conditions.
  • Ocean discount rate (default 12%) reflects the higher risk premium appropriate for a first-of-kind marine infrastructure program vs. established terrestrial construction.

  • GPUs not included — models everything upstream of compute hardware.
  • Capacity factor 85%; fuel price held flat; no carbon price, hedging, or escalation modeled.
  • dc_infra_per_W covers the facility (electrical, mechanical, civil/shell, fit-out) at the stated $/W; gas turbine capex is a separate line scaled by PUE.
  • Terrestrial discount rate (default 8%) reflects the lower risk of proven technology and established financing markets for CCGT + datacenter builds.

Wave Power

The theoretical power per meter of wave crest in deep water:

P = (ρg²/64π) · H_s² · T_e

ρ = 1025 kg/m³ (seawater), g = 9.81 m/s², Hs = significant wave height (m), Te = energy period (s). Result is watts per meter of crest width.

Worked example. At a North Pacific site (Hs ≈ 3 m, Te ≈ 9 s): P ≈ 70 kW/m. With a ~15 m effective capture width and ~40% wave-to-wire efficiency, single-node rated power ≈ 420 kW. Reaching 1 MW per node requires either premium sites (Hs ≈ 4–5 m) or larger capture width. The arithmetic is straightforward — rated MW scales as capture_width × efficiency × P(Hs, Te).

Heat Rejection

Seawater is a near-infinite cold reservoir at 5–15 °C with a convective heat transfer coefficient h ≈ 1000 W/m²·K. The orbital radiator constraint that dominates McCalip's analysis — where vacuum forces all heat rejection through radiation, imposing a strict area-density limit on the entire design — is absent here. Seawater convection collapses the thermal sub-problem to a constant. This is the single largest engineering tailwind the ocean case has over the orbital case.

Biofouling, Corrosion, and Survival

  • Coatings. Antifouling hull coatings (biocide or foul-release) reduce growth rates; cleaning cadence ≈ 6–24 months depending on sea area.
  • Electronics enclosures. Hermetic sealed enclosures isolate compute payload from marine atmosphere. Standard practice in offshore oil & gas.
  • Corrosion. Cathodic protection + coatings for steel/composite structures; electronics inside hermetic enclosures.
  • Survival loading. Structural design to 100-year-wave criteria; dynamic mooring analysis. Not in the cost model — captured implicitly in the hull/node manufacturing cost.
